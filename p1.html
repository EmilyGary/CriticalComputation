Lost and Found - Mood Ring

Description

This project is a depiction of the below description given by my colleague about a lost item: "I once had a mood ring I got at a carnival, but I lost it somewhere along the way. It was a thin gold band with an oval stone in the center. The stone’s color was somewhere between black and deep green. Sometimes the stone’s color turned purple, blue, red, orange, or yellow. I miss it.

Design Process

This project began with an exercise where my colleague Alex Modlin and I exchanged brief descriptions about an item we had lost in the past. With the above description, I first began to draw basic sketches of the mood ring to decide what view of it I wanted to depict. As I began sketching, I found that the story of the mood ring became increasingly important in order to personalize the depiction of the lost item.

Functionally, I first began to draw directly in p5js, however had to take a step back as I realized that sketching would be a crucial step in translating the image into code. I attempted to draw the ring using basic shapes in order to simplify my mental model of the code required. The sketches were critical in determining which layers each element of the lost item belonged in since p5js drawing occurs from bottom to top layers unlike when drawing freehand. Assigning color to the work was less methodical and I played with different color combinations once the basic shapes were drawn out. I went a step further to include an interactive element to highlight the changing colors that Alex mentioned in his description of the mood ring.

Reflection

Alex's description of the lost item provided a variety of colors, which I wanted to include in my own depiction of the mood ring. The combination of the carnival setting and a mood ring gave me a sense of nostalgia, which I wanted to include by creating a classic image of a carnival tent with red and white stripes to provide context. I chose to leave the mood ring floating in space to highlight the wonder of where it ultimately may have ended up - neither here nor there. Both the mood ring and the setting were whimsical in Alex's description of the item. Inspired by the ever-changing aesthetic of mood rings, I wanted to mirror this in the work by making it interactive and displaying a wide range of color schemes. The cover photo portrays an air of mysticism and dark colors to note the loss, while the interactive display of the work gives a nod to the playfulness of mood rings and the carnival setting where it ultimately became a relic of a simpler time.
