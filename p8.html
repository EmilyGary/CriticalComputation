Experimental Camera - Brain Storm

Center yourself in the camera. When you get an idea, say "ah-hah!"

Description

Sometimes the most difficult part of starting a new project is coming up with the idea. This project can be used as both a tool and a distraction when the well has run dry on good ideas. The design stems from my own struggles as a graduate student in design. I spend a lot of time brainstorming for projects, but the process of brainstorming can be so abstract that sometimes I feel guilty for "not doing anything". This experimental camera brings movement to the process of brainstorming in an attempt to alleviate the guilt that many of us have when we can't see physical results from our mental efforts. In creative practices, the concept is often the most important part. Whether your ideation is helpful or procrastination with better branding, the Brain Storm gives us something to show for it.

Design Process

Originally, I sketched out some ideas that would transform the viewer into something else. The first three concepts were: 1. Display color in terms of molecules using the 4 molecules people are comprised of (hydrogen, carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen). 2. Overlay a map over the silhouette of the person to make a globe out of them. 3. Replace particular facial features with coins or money. However, I realized that these were missing interactivity, which is an important part of a filtered camera sketch. As I began brainstorming, I was inspired to create the Brain Storm camera. I created the clouds and lightning first, and played with different offsets to create a storm-like effect that doesn't cover the user's face. After the brain storm was complete, I sketched the lightbulb. I then tested how far apart the two lip points of the facial recognition need to be in order to detect someone saying "ah-hah!" I settled on a distance of 15 pixels, and realized as I was testing that the nose and eye features perfectly drew the wire component of the lightbulb. I chose to use this component of the face within the lightbulb to showcase the user as they are having their "ah-ha moment".

Reflection

This assignment pulled back the curtain on the interactive filters which are ubiquitous in social media. It was both exciting and alarming to see how quickly and easily facial recognition can be implemented. In my case, the intention was primarily entertainment, but even my decisions in determining the distance between the lips while saying "ah-ha" could impact my users as they use the Brain Storm. I can easily see how these same sorts of judgement calls could lead to much graver outcomes when they are made for facial recognition for identification or security purposes as there was variability in this "critical distance" even when testing among a few friends. I also learned that for interactive sketches, testing is important and may generate new ideas for the final project along the way. I hadn't realized I could use the facial tracing as the interior of the lightbulb until I was testing the "ah-ha" moment.
